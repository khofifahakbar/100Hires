We deliberately made a client post look ugly.

58k impressions. (Our designer was pissed…)

We have a full design team at distinctiva.io. Some of the most talented creatives I've ever worked with, and they've built the visual identity behind some of the biggest creators on this platform. 

But when your client is a co-founder, investor, and venture partner running a 200-person dev agency - a branded carousel signals the wrong thing entirely. 

And for this post, we told his designated designer to sit this one out.

She wasn't happy. She couldn't believe we were going to deliver that:

A photo, a red X, a green checkmark, a logo slapped on.

It outperformed every other post.

An 8k-follower account. Hitting 58,666k impressions.

There's a real psychological principle behind this:

the pratfall effect.

When someone competent shows a small flaw, people trust them more. The imperfection makes them relatable.

That's LinkedIn in 2026.

People's brains have gotten tired of the "just because" polished designs that ruled LinkedIn in 2022. And when they spot it now, they scroll right past.

And the data backs it: image climbing in strong-tier reach three months straight, text sitting at an 83x breakout multiple, carousel's typical reach dropping while its ceiling rises. per andreas jonsson

This doesn't mean scrapping design entirely. We're more strategic with it than ever at Distinctiva. Every client gets a different answer.

Every format is polarizing - the middle is shrinking, the top is pulling away.

Every design has a job, and it shows.

The order matters more than anything: start with something real, then apply the hook, the structure, the funnel mapping.

Ugly on purpose, strategic underneath.
