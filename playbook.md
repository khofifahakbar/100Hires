## Section: Where experts disagree

### a) Topic: Posting Frequency

**Experts:** Lara Acosta, Michelle J Raymond  

**Recommendation 1:**  
"Start with posting 3x per week is more effective, especially early on. Posting fewer but higher-quality pieces is better.”  
Source: Lara Acosta, 23 September 2025  
Link to post: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=r7nnmWBTHUk&t=135s  
Date: 23 September 2025  

**Recommendation 2:**  
“If you’re new or have a small audience, start with 1 post per week."  
Source: Michelle J Raymond  
Link to post: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5zOK-ju1s8b4  
Date: 18 February 2025  

**Which side I take:** I side with Lara Acosta.

**Why?**  
Posting only once a week (Michelle J Raymond) for a founder-led organic content is not enough to get noticed in a busy industry like recruiting. However, trying to post every day is too difficult for a small team. Three times a week is the green zone because it keeps 100Hires visible without sacrificing the quality of the posts.

**How to implement for 100Hires?**  
See Mr. Kravets' analytics on which day his visitors are most active and choose three set days (e.g., Tuesday, Wednesday, and Thursday). On these days, Mr. Kravets will share one useful tip for the target audience. This creates a routine that the audience can count on.

**Experts:** Diandra Escobar, Chris Donnelly  

**Recommendation 1:**  
“Focus on educational content, sharing frameworks, step-by-step guides, and actionable knowledge are the highest-performing content formats on LinkedIn for engagement and follower conversion."  
Source: Chris Donnelly  
Link to post: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=CM_HDRF4y0s&t=344s  
Date: 18 February 2026  

**Recommendation 2:**  
"Namejacking or pointing out influential people your audience already follows as a pattern interrupt to get attention by delivering a personal perspective related to their actions, statements, or philosophy."  
Source: Diandra Escobar  
Link to post: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=twBrrKkhpOg&t=111s  
Date: 21 March 2026  

**Which side I take:** I side with Chris Donnelly

**Why?**  
Talking about famous people (Diandra Escobar’s idea, which she claims to be “The 4 Best Post Types to BLOW UP on LinkedIn”) is a quick way to get people to look at you, but it doesn't prove that your product is actually good. Since 100Hires is a professional tool for businesses, we want people to respect our expertise through Mr.Kravets’ LinkedIn. Sharing educational content more proves that 100Hires actually understands the hiring industry and can provide real value to other companies.

**How to implement for 100Hires?**  
We will identify the three biggest pain points, such as ending up with bad candidates that led to cost inefficiency or slow hiring processes, and create simple, bite-sized guides, frameworks, tips, and tricks to fix them. These will be shared as easy-to-read documents that people can save and use later.

### c) Topic: LinkedIn profile optimization

**Experts:** Tommy Clark, Pierre Herubel  

**Recommendation 1:**  
“Treat your LinkedIn profile like a landing page optimized to convert visitors into followers and leads. First thing, a professional headshot for your profile picture, close-up (shoulder and above), avoid AI images and selfies. Next, the header image can be branded or scrappier. The headline should be a simple, clear one-liner: role + value proposition (e.g., “CEO at Company - helping B2B SaaS grow”). The about section should be a concise, clear summary of what you do, who you serve, social proof, and a call-to-action (CTA). In the featured link, include 1-2 links. One to homepage/demo, one to newsletter or lead magnet. Use LinkedIn Premium to add a custom button linking to a newsletter or lead magnet if possible."  
Source: Tommy Clark  
Link to post: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0EA0n1Ka41k&t=2s  
Date: 3 January 2026  

**Recommendation 2:**  
"First impressions are critical; the profile photo is the first element people notice. Often underutilized, the banner is a valuable space to visually reinforce your personal brand and value proposition. The headline is not just your job title, nor should it be a hard sales pitch. It should clearly communicate what you do, who you help, and the outcome you deliver. In the “about section,” tell your story and demonstrate how you help your audience. Focus on who you help, what problems you solve, ideally using the Problem-Agitate-Solution (PAS) framework; present the problem, agitate by describing symptoms and side effects, and offer your solution. Continue it with why you are uniquely qualified, your approach or proprietary method, and clear next steps (how to contact you, book a call, or buy your offer). Choose between a direct approach (clear answers to questions) or a storytelling approach (share a discovery story to trigger engagement).  
Use simple, jargon-free language.”  
Source: Pierre Herubel  
Link to post: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3sf8fojVfpM  
Date: 23 June 2025  

**Which side I take:** Pierre Herubel

**Why?**  
Tommy Clark suggests the top banner can be "scrappy." For the Mr. Kravets of 100Hires, this is a mistake. When a high-level manager or a recruiter visits Mr. Kravets’ page, they need to feel immediate trust. Pierre’s method turns the profile into a professional landing page that proves Mr. Kravets is an authority. A simple, brief profile might look like Mr. Kravets doesn't have much to say, but a detailed one shows they deeply understand the hiring industry.

**How to implement for 100Hires?**  
1. The Banner: Instead of a generic picture, we will use the 100Hires' and add 3–5 well-known companies' logos already using the software. This builds instant trust.  
2. The About Section: We will write this like a short letter to recruiters. We’ll start by mentioning the #1 thing they hate (like spending hours on bad resumes), explain why that is costing them money, and then show how Mr. Kravets' unique software fixes it.  
3. The Headline: Change it from just "CEO at 100Hires" to adding something that promises a result, like: "Helping recruiters cut their hiring time in half."

## Section: What I rejected and why

1) Michelle states that growth is "human before corporate." If the goal is to be human, it is more effective for Mr. Kravets to post from his personal profile. People naturally trust a founder’s face and voice more than a company logo. By focusing on the personal brand first, we skip the difficult step of trying to "humanize" a faceless company page.  
LinkedIn’s algorithm also prioritizes personal posts over company posts. If we spend 5 hours a week on the company page, we might get 1K views. If we spend those same 5 hours on Mr. Kravets’ personal profile, we could get 10K views. It is much smarter to build Mr. Kravets’ authority first and let the company page exist simply as a "verified business card" in the background.

## Section: My original ideas

**Idea Name:** The "10-Minute Talent Race" (Manual vs. Machine)

**The Concept:**  
Mr. Kravets creates a split-screen video. On the left side, he records a manual hiring process as a startup founder trying to hire the old-fashioned way: opening multiple browser tabs, scrolling through a messy email inbox, and reading every single resume one by one. On the right side, he shows the 100Hires’ dashboard performing the same task autonomously.

**What makes it unique and relevant:**  
To make this interesting and credible, Mr. Kravets adds a "Cost-of-Delay" by putting a clock at the bottom of the screen. As the manual hiring process burdens “the startup CEO”, the clock shows how much money is being "wasted" in recruiter hours and lost productivity in each step. The 100Hires’ side stops the clock before other “startup founder” doing manual hiring keeps ticking for a longer time.

**The Content Framework:**  

1. The Setup (The Hook)  
Mr. Kravets starts with a bold claim like "I’m going to hire a Senior Senior Sales Lead. Let’s see how long it will take to find the best one.” (clock starts)  

2. The Middle (The Technical Proof):  
● The Left Screen (manual process): Mr. Kravets is shown getting overwhelmed looking at a resume that is 5 pages long. He’s looking for a specific skill (e.g., "Experience with SaaS contracts"). It takes him 4 minutes just to find that one line.  
● The Right Screen (100Hires): Mr. Kravets shows the AI-ranking feature. He clicks a button, and the software instantly highlights that specific skill across 100 applicants and ranks them by who has the most experience. (clock ends)  
● The Unique Moment: The CEO pauses the video and says, "Look at Candidate #42. That guy (pointing to the left) would have rejected them because their resume was formatted poorly. But the AI saw through the mess and found they have the exact technical skill we need. We just found the 'hidden gem' I would have missed."  

3. The Climax  
The 100Hires side finishes in under 5 minutes with 3 interviews automatically booked into the calendar. The "Manual" side is still on candidate #5. The CEO stops the clock and shows the final "Bill":  
● Manual: 4 hours of Founder time ($1,000 value).  
● 100Hires: 5 minutes of AI time ($2 value).  

4. The Conclusion (The CTA):  
"Hiring isn't about reading paper; it’s about knowing people. 100Hires does the 'paper' so you can know the 'people.'"

**Why this works (The Logic):**  
● It explains the frequent struggle: It uses a visual "race" to explain complex automation. Everyone understands a race.  
● It addresses the "trust" gap: By showing the AI find a "hidden gem" that a tired human missed, it proves that 100Hires isn't just "fast," it’s accurate.  
● It targets the founder's ego: Founders pride themselves on being efficient. Showing them they are actually "the bottlenecks" is a powerful emotional trigger that drives them to try the software.

**The tools needed:**  
Mr. Kravets can use a simple screen-recording tool (like Loom) and a free video editor to put the two clips side-by-side. This costs $0 to produce but creates high-authority, proven video content.

## Section: Weaknesses of this playbook

### 1. The risks and the solution

a) Posting Frequency (Lara Acosta’s Approach)  
By choosing to post only three times per week, the primary risk is algorithmic invisibility. In the fast-moving LinkedIn environment of 2026, posting less frequently than competitors who may be posting most often could mean 100Hires is pushed out of the audience's feed. On the contrary, Mr. Kravets can be too packed, and we aren’t planning to sacrifice quality for quantity. To fix this, we should actively engage. On the days when Mr. Kravets is not publishing a post, it’s recommended to spend at least 15 minutes commenting on 5–10 relevant posts in our niche from target startup founders, hiring managers, and industry leaders to remain visible in their radar without having to create a full post.  

b) Content Format (Chris Donnelly’s Approach)  
Siding with educational content and frameworks creates a risk of being “too stiff.” Strictly educational content can sometimes feel too formal or dry, which might fail to capture attention as quickly as "namejacking" influential people. While education builds authority, it can lack an emotional hook. Therefore, we should anchor every educational guide in a real-world "pain point". Starting each post with a specific story about a hiring failure ensures the content stays human and interesting while still being helpful.  

c) Profile Optimization (Pierre Herubel’s Approach)  
Choosing a detailed, long-form “about section” might appear as a large "wall of text" on a smartphone screen, causing busy recruiters to scroll past the important parts. Thus, we can dodge this by making people scan, not read. Within the detailed section, Mr. Kravets should use short paragraphs (2-3 sentences max) and bullet points so that even a visitor who only looks at the page for three seconds can understand what 100Hires does and offers.  

### 2. Untested Assumptions

a) Founder Capacity and Skill: The most significant untested assumption is Mr. Kravets’ ability and willingness to consistently produce technical video content like the "Hiring A Senior Talent Race". This idea assumes he has the technical comfort to record his screen and the time to narrate these comparisons regularly.  

b) Audience Appetite for Depth: We are assuming that small business and startup founders actually prefer "Educational Frameworks" over more sensationalized content that grabs attention at a glance. While we believe depth builds more trust, it is untested whether this style will generate enough initial engagement to reach new people.  

c) Perception of AI Ranking: We are assuming that the target audience will see the "AI-Ranking" feature as the “star feature,” a helpful, accurate tool for them. Some professionals are still skeptical of AI making hiring decisions (because a lot of companies are still partnered with hiring agencies), and we have not yet tested whether showing the technology "beating" a human in a race is worth their attention or not.  

### 3. What is Missing?

a) Comment Management: The playbook explains what to post, but lacks a plan for what happens after the post is live. There is no protocol for how to handle replies or how to move someone from a comment into a sales conversation.  

b) Proactive Connection: The current plan is entirely focused on "Inbound" traffic (people coming to the profile). It is missing a strategy for "Outbound" growth, such as how Mr. Kravets should use his optimized profile to send personalized connection requests to the specific company founders he wants as clients.  

c) Lead Capture: The playbook is missing a “lead magnet strategy." This would be a specific, downloadable resource, like a PDF version of the hiring guides, that allows 100Hires to collect email addresses so they can continue the conversation outside of LinkedIn.
